# ChronoCarta — Suggested Starter Maps

Each regional theme produces multiple maps with distinct fingerprints. The player can't just recognize the region — they have to read the specific combination of names, borders, and political entities to pin down the time slice. Eleven themes, 50 maps, spanning the Bronze Age to the 1990s.

---

## 1. Post-Napoleonic Europe (Year Precision)

The political map of Europe changed rapidly between 1806 and 1848. Each of these four maps has a different combination of entities.

**~1806.** The Holy Roman Empire has just been dissolved. Napoleon's Confederation of the Rhine dominates central Europe. Prussia is diminished after Jena. The Kingdom of Italy is a Napoleonic client state. Spain is still an ally of France (it doesn't revolt until 1808).

**~1812.** Napoleon's empire at maximum extent. The Illyrian Provinces run down the Adriatic coast. The Grand Duchy of Warsaw exists. Spain is now in revolt — the Peninsular War is ongoing. The Papal States have been annexed by France.

**~1815.** Congress of Vienna settlement. France pushed back to 1792 borders. Kingdom of the Netherlands includes Belgium. German Confederation replaces the Confederation of the Rhine. The Papal States are restored. Norway is joined to Sweden. Poland is partitioned — the Congress Kingdom exists under Russian control.

**~1831.** Belgium has separated from the Netherlands (1830). The Congress Kingdom of Poland has been absorbed after the November Uprising. Greece is independent (1829). Otherwise similar to 1815 — German Confederation still intact, Italy still fragmented.

## 2. Post-Colonial Africa (Decade Precision)

African country names changed in waves. Each map in this series has a unique fingerprint of colonial vs. independent names.

**~1960.** The "Year of Africa" — 17 countries gain independence. French West Africa and French Equatorial Africa have just dissolved into individual nations. The Congo is independent but chaotic. Tanganyika is not yet independent (1961). Southern Rhodesia, Northern Rhodesia, and Nyasaland still exist as the Central African Federation. South West Africa (Namibia) is still under South African control. Portuguese colonies (Angola, Mozambique, Guinea-Bissau) remain.

**~1970.** Tanzania exists (Tanganyika + Zanzibar merged 1964). Malawi has replaced Nyasaland (1964). Zambia has replaced Northern Rhodesia (1964). Rhodesia has made its Unilateral Declaration of Independence (1965). Congo is not yet Zaire (that's 1971). Biafra may appear on some maps (Nigerian Civil War 1967–1970). Portuguese colonies still hold.

**~1976.** Zaire has replaced Congo (1971). Angola and Mozambique are independent (1975). Guinea-Bissau is independent (1974). Rhodesia still exists. Upper Volta still exists. Djibouti is still French Territory of the Afars and Issas (independent 1977).

**~1985.** Zimbabwe has replaced Rhodesia (1980). Burkina Faso has replaced Upper Volta (1984). Djibouti is independent. But Namibia is still South West Africa (independent 1990). Eritrea is still part of Ethiopia (independent 1993). Western Sahara's status is contested.

## 3. Soviet Collapse and European Redrawing (Year Precision)

The highest density of overlapping changes in modern cartographic history. Some of these maps are distinguishable to a six-month window.

**~1989.** The Berlin Wall has fallen but Germany is not yet unified. The USSR is intact. Yugoslavia is intact. Czechoslovakia is intact. All three Baltic states are still Soviet republics. Eritrea is part of Ethiopia.

**~1991 (early).** Germany is unified (October 1990). The USSR still exists but the Baltics are asserting independence. Yugoslavia is starting to fracture — Slovenia and Croatia declare independence in June 1991. Czechoslovakia is still one country.

**~1992.** The USSR has dissolved (December 1991) — 15 successor states visible. Slovenia and Croatia are independent. Bosnia is in the process of declaring independence. Czechoslovakia is still one country (splits January 1993). Eritrea is still part of Ethiopia.

**~1994.** Czechoslovakia has split into Czech Republic and Slovakia (January 1993). Eritrea is independent (1993). The former Yugoslavia shows Slovenia, Croatia, Bosnia, and rump Yugoslavia (Serbia and Montenegro). All former Soviet republics are independent states.

## 4. Americas Independence Cascade (Decade Precision)

Independence movements swept the Americas from north to south over half a century, each on its own timeline.

**~1810.** Haiti is independent (1804) — the only independent former colony in the Caribbean. The United States exists but its western boundary is the Mississippi (Louisiana Purchase happened but the territory isn't states yet). All of Spanish America is still colonial — Gran Colombia, Peru, Mexico, La Plata are all under Spanish control. Brazil is Portuguese. British, French, and Dutch colonies dot the Caribbean.

**~1825.** Mexico is independent (1821) and includes Central America and Texas. Gran Colombia exists as a single entity (Venezuela, Colombia, Ecuador, Panama). Peru and Bolivia are independent. Argentina (United Provinces of the Río de la Plata) is independent. Brazil is an independent empire (1822). Chile is independent (1818). Paraguay and Uruguay are independent.

**~1835.** Gran Colombia has dissolved (1831) — Venezuela, Ecuador, and New Granada are separate. Central America has separated from Mexico and exists as the Federal Republic of Central America (1823–1841). Texas is an independent republic (1836). Brazil is still an empire.

**~1850.** The Federal Republic of Central America has dissolved into Guatemala, El Salvador, Honduras, Nicaragua, and Costa Rica. Texas is a US state (1845). The US-Mexico War has redrawn the border (1848) — the US now extends to the Pacific. California is a state (1850). Cuba and Puerto Rico are still Spanish.

## 5. Southeast Asian Decolonization (Decade Precision)

Each country decolonized on its own schedule, creating a different map fingerprint every few years.

**~1948.** Burma is independent (1948). Indonesia is fighting for independence but is still officially the Dutch East Indies (recognized 1949). French Indochina is intact — Vietnam, Laos, and Cambodia are all under French control. Malaya is a British colony. The Philippines are independent (1946). North and South Korea are newly divided (1948).

**~1955.** Indonesia is independent. French Indochina has fallen — Vietnam is partitioned at the 17th parallel into North and South (1954), Laos and Cambodia are independent. Malaya is still a British colony (independent 1957). Singapore is a separate British Crown Colony.

**~1965.** Malaya has become Malaysia by merging with Singapore, Sabah, and Sarawak (1963). But Singapore has just been expelled (1965) — if Singapore is separate from Malaysia, it's 1965 or later. North Borneo has been renamed Sabah. East Timor is still Portuguese (Indonesia invades 1975).

**~1976.** South Vietnam no longer exists — reunified as the Socialist Republic of Vietnam (1975). East Timor has been annexed by Indonesia (1975–1976). Cambodia is under Khmer Rouge control (renamed Democratic Kampuchea). Laos has become the Lao People's Democratic Republic (1975). Burma is still Burma (renamed Myanmar 1989).

## 6. Bronze Age and Ancient Near East (Century Precision)

The eastern Mediterranean in the Bronze Age was a world of competing great powers — Egypt, the Hittites, Mitanni, Babylon, Assyria, Mycenaean Greece — and the status of each is an independent clock. The Bronze Age Collapse (~1177 BC) is the sharpest dividing line in ancient history: before it, great palace civilizations; after it, a dark age from which new powers slowly emerge. Old Testament geography maps onto the later slices.

**~1450 BC.** The Egyptian New Kingdom at its height under Thutmose III — Egypt controls Canaan and extends influence into Syria. The Mitanni kingdom controls upper Mesopotamia and northern Syria (key tell: if Mitanni exists, it's before ~1300 BC). The Hittite Empire is rising in Anatolia but not yet a superpower. Mycenaean Greece is flourishing on the mainland. Minoan Crete is declining after the Thera eruption. Kassite Babylon controls southern Mesopotamia. Assyria exists but is a vassal of Mitanni.

**~1250 BC.** The age of Ramesses II. Egypt and the Hittites are the two superpowers — the Battle of Kadesh (1274 BC) has been fought, and they've signed a peace treaty. Mitanni is gone — destroyed and partitioned between the Hittites and Assyrians (key tell: Mitanni's absence). Assyria is now independent and growing. Mycenaean Greece is at its height — Troy still stands. Ugarit is a thriving coastal city. The entire eastern Mediterranean is interconnected by trade. If the Hittites and Egypt both appear as great powers but Mitanni does not, it's the late 13th century BC.

**~1100 BC.** The Bronze Age Collapse has happened. The Hittite Empire is gone — completely destroyed (key tell: no Hittites means post-1178 BC). Mycenaean palace civilization has collapsed. The Sea Peoples have settled — the Philistines now occupy the southern Canaanite coast. Egypt has retreated to the Nile Valley, losing control of Canaan. Ugarit has been destroyed. Assyria has retreated and weakened. The Israelite tribes are settling the central Canaanite highlands. If the Hittites are absent and the Philistines are present, it's the post-collapse era.

**~950 BC.** The Old Testament united monarchy — David and Solomon's kingdom controls the land from Dan to Beersheba, with vassal states in Edom, Moab, and Ammon. The Philistines are contained to a coastal strip. Phoenician city-states (Tyre, Sidon) are at their commercial peak — Solomon's alliance with Hiram of Tyre is active. Egypt is weak and divided (Third Intermediate Period). Assyria is in a period of decline. No major empires threaten the region — it's the power vacuum that makes the Israelite kingdom possible. If Israel is a unified kingdom and no great empire dominates the region, it's the 10th century BC.

**~720 BC.** The kingdom has split — Israel in the north and Judah in the south (divided ~930 BC). But the Neo-Assyrian Empire is now the dominant superpower, and Israel has just been conquered and destroyed (722 BC). If Israel is gone but Judah survives as an Assyrian vassal, it's the late 8th century. Phoenician cities are Assyrian vassals. Egypt is reunifying under the Kushite/Nubian 25th Dynasty. Babylon is under Assyrian control.

**~560 BC.** Judah has been conquered by Nebuchadnezzar's Neo-Babylonian Empire (586 BC) — the Temple is destroyed, the Babylonian exile is underway. Assyria is gone (fell 609 BC). The Neo-Babylonian Empire controls Mesopotamia, Syria, and the Levant. Egypt is independent but weakened. The Median Empire controls Persia and parts of Anatolia. Lydia controls western Anatolia. If Babylon is the dominant power and Assyria is absent, it's the 6th century BC. If the Persian Empire appears instead of Babylon, it's after 539 BC (Cyrus's conquest).

## 7. The Greek World and the Peloponnesian War (Decade Precision)

The political map of the Greek world reshuffled repeatedly between the Persian Wars and the rise of Macedon. The cross-reference features are alliance systems, which city-states are independent versus subject, who holds Ionia, whether Persia is involved, and the status of key cities. A reader of Thucydides can practically date these from memory.

**~475 BC.** Post-Persian Wars. The Delian League has just been founded under Athenian leadership — the treasury is still at Delos (moved to Athens ~454 BC). Sparta leads the Peloponnesian League but has withdrawn from overseas operations. The Ionian Greek cities are liberated from Persia and joining the Delian League. Themistocles is building the Long Walls. Thebes is weakened — it had medized during the Persian invasion. Syracuse is the dominant power in Sicily under Hiero I. If the Delian League exists but feels like a genuine alliance of equals rather than an Athenian empire, it's the early decades.

**~435 BC.** The Athenian Empire at its height. The Delian League treasury has been moved to Athens — the alliance has become an empire. Tribute-paying subject states ring the Aegean. Pericles dominates Athenian politics. Corinth and Athens are clashing over Corcyra and Potidaea. Sparta and its allies are preparing for war. Aegina has been subjugated. Plataea is still an independent Athenian ally. Melos is still independent. If Athens controls the Aegean but war hasn't started, it's the 430s.

**~420 BC.** The Archidamian War has been fought. The plague has devastated Athens (430–426 BC). Plataea has been destroyed by Thebes (427 BC). Mytilene revolted and was crushed (428–427 BC). Pylos is in Athenian hands (425 BC). Brasidas has captured Amphipolis in Thrace (424 BC). The Peace of Nicias has paused the war (421 BC). Argos has allied with Athens. Melos is about to be destroyed (416 BC) or has just been — if Melos is absent, it's after 416 BC. The Sicilian expedition hasn't happened yet.

**~410 BC.** Catastrophe. The Sicilian expedition has been destroyed (413 BC) — the Athenian fleet and army annihilated at Syracuse. Sparta has fortified Decelea in Attica as a permanent base. Athenian allies in Ionia are revolting. Persia is now funding the Spartan fleet through Tissaphernes and Pharnabazus. The oligarchic coup of the Four Hundred has come and gone (411 BC). Alcibiades is fighting for Athens again from exile. Athens is still in the war but visibly losing. If Persia is backing Sparta and Ionian allies are defecting, it's the final phase.

**~400 BC.** The war is over. Athens surrendered (404 BC). The Long Walls are demolished. The Thirty Tyrants ruled briefly and have been overthrown (403 BC). Spartan hegemony — Lysander has installed oligarchies across the Greek world. But Corinth and Thebes are already dissatisfied with Sparta. The Persian Empire has reasserted control over the Ionian Greek cities (part of the deal for funding Sparta). If Sparta dominates Greece but former allies are restless, it's the immediate post-war years.

**~365 BC.** Spartan hegemony is broken. Thebes under Epaminondas has won at Leuctra (371 BC) — the Spartan army's aura of invincibility is shattered. Messenia has been liberated after centuries of Spartan helot slavery — Messene is founded as an independent city (369 BC). The Second Athenian League exists but is weaker than the first. Arcadia has unified. Thebes is the strongest land power in Greece but its hegemony is fragile and dependent on Epaminondas personally. If Messenia is free and Thebes dominates, it's the 360s. Macedon under Philip II is rising in the north but not yet a major player.

## 8. The Roman World (Century Precision)

The Roman state changed form and extent over a thousand years. Each time slice has a distinct territorial fingerprint.

**~100 BC.** The Roman Republic controls Italy, Spain, North Africa (after Carthage's destruction in 146 BC), Greece, and parts of Asia Minor. But Gaul is not yet Roman (Caesar's conquest is 58–50 BC). Egypt is still Ptolemaic. Britain is unconquered. The eastern Mediterranean is a patchwork of client kingdoms and Hellenistic successor states. No division between east and west.

**~117 AD.** The Empire at maximum extent under Trajan. Mesopotamia is briefly Roman (Trajan's Parthian campaign). Dacia (modern Romania) is a province (conquered 106 AD). Britain is partially conquered — Hadrian's Wall hasn't been built yet (122 AD). Egypt is a Roman province. The entire Mediterranean is a Roman lake.

**~300 AD.** Diocletian's Tetrarchy — the Empire is administratively divided into four prefectures but still nominally unified. Dacia has been abandoned (270s). Britain is still Roman. The capital has shifted — Rome is no longer the primary seat of power. Mesopotamia is back in Sasanian Persian hands.

**~450 AD.** The Western Empire is collapsing. Britain has been abandoned (410). Vandals control North Africa (captured Carthage 439). Visigoths hold much of Spain and southern Gaul. Italy is still nominally Roman but controlled by Germanic generals. The Eastern Empire (Byzantine) is intact and relatively strong, controlling the Balkans, Anatolia, Egypt, and the Levant.

## 9. The Byzantine Empire (Century Precision)

The Byzantine state persisted for a thousand years but its territorial extent fluctuated wildly. Egypt, Anatolia, Bulgaria, and southern Italy were each held and lost on independent timelines, creating a distinct fingerprint at each time slice. Overlaps chronologically with the Crusader States series but focuses from Constantinople outward rather than from the Levant.

**~550 AD.** Justinian's reconquests have brought the empire to its maximum medieval extent. Italy is recovered from the Ostrogoths. North Africa is recovered from the Vandals. Southern Spain is Byzantine. Egypt, Syria, and Anatolia are all securely held. The Balkans are threatened by Slavic and Avar raids but still imperial territory. If Italy and North Africa are Byzantine, it's Justinian's era.

**~700 AD.** The Arab conquests have transformed the map. Egypt is gone (642). Syria and the Levant are gone (636–640). North Africa is gone or falling. Southern Spain is gone. But Anatolia is still Byzantine — the Taurus Mountains are the frontier. Southern Italy and Sicily are still held. Bulgaria is emerging as an independent khanate in the Balkans (681). The empire is dramatically smaller but cohesive. If Egypt is Arab but Anatolia is Byzantine, it's this window.

**~1025 AD.** The empire at its second peak under Basil II. Bulgaria has been conquered (1018) — the entire Balkans are Byzantine. Anatolia is secure and extends to the Armenian highlands. Southern Italy is still held. But Egypt and Syria are Fatimid, not Byzantine. Crete and Cyprus have been recovered from the Arabs. If Bulgaria is Byzantine and Anatolia is secure but Egypt is Fatimid, it's the early 11th century.

**~1100 AD.** Post-Manzikert (1071) collapse. Most of Anatolia has been lost to the Seljuk Sultanate of Rum — the empire retains only the western coastal fringe. Southern Italy has been lost to the Normans (completed 1071). Bulgaria is still Byzantine. The Crusader states have just appeared in the Levant. Constantinople is secure. If Anatolia is mostly Seljuk but Constantinople and the Balkans are intact, it's the late 11th or early 12th century.

**~1215 AD.** The Fourth Crusade has shattered the empire (1204). Constantinople is held by the Latin Empire. The Byzantine successor states — the Empire of Nicaea in western Anatolia, the Empire of Trebizond on the Black Sea coast, and the Despotate of Epirus in western Greece — compete to reclaim the capital. The Second Bulgarian Empire is independent (since 1185). Crusader principalities dot Greece (Duchy of Athens, Principality of Achaea). If Constantinople is Latin and multiple Byzantine successor states exist, it's 1204–1261.

**~1350 AD.** The restored empire (Constantinople recovered 1261) is a shadow. Territory is reduced to Constantinople, Thessaloniki, parts of the Peloponnese (Morea), and scattered Aegean islands. The Ottoman Turks control most of Anatolia and are crossing into Europe. Stefan Dušan's Serbian Empire dominates the western Balkans. The Second Bulgarian Empire is weakening. Trebizond survives as an independent Greek state on the Black Sea. If the empire is confined to a few cities while Ottomans hold Anatolia and Serbs hold the Balkans, it's the mid-14th century.

## 10. The Crusader States and Eastern Mediterranean (Century Precision)

The Crusader states rose and fell over two centuries, each one on its own timeline. The surrounding Islamic powers also changed, giving multiple cross-reference points.

**~1100 AD.** All four Crusader states exist — the County of Edessa, Principality of Antioch, County of Tripoli, and Kingdom of Jerusalem. The Fatimid Caliphate controls Egypt. The Seljuk Sultanate of Rum holds central Anatolia. The Byzantine Empire still controls western Anatolia and the Balkans. The Islamic world is fragmented between competing Seljuk successor states.

**~1150 AD.** The County of Edessa has fallen (1144) — its absence is the single most dateable feature. The other three Crusader states survive. Zengi's successor Nur ad-Din controls Aleppo and Damascus (unified 1154). The Fatimids still hold Egypt but are weakening. The Byzantine Empire under Manuel I is actively involved in the region.

**~1190 AD.** Saladin has conquered Jerusalem (1187). The Kingdom of Jerusalem is reduced to a coastal strip centered on Acre. The Ayyubid dynasty controls Egypt and much of the Levant. The County of Tripoli and Principality of Antioch survive as diminished remnants. The Third Crusade has just partially restored the coastal kingdom. The Byzantine Empire has lost territory to the Seljuks.

**~1250 AD.** The Latin Empire of Constantinople exists (Fourth Crusade captured Constantinople in 1204) or has just fallen (Byzantines recapture it in 1261). The Crusader states are thin coastal strips. The Ayyubid dynasty has been overthrown by the Mamluks in Egypt (1250). The Mongol Empire is pressing into the region from the east — the Ilkhanate controls Persia and Mesopotamia.

## 11. Carolingian and Post-Carolingian Europe (Century Precision)

Charlemagne's empire unified and then fragmented western Europe. Each partition created a different map, and the successor states evolved on independent timelines.

**~800 AD.** Charlemagne's empire at its height after his coronation as Emperor. A single Frankish realm stretches from the Pyrenees to the Elbe, from the North Sea to central Italy. The Papal States exist as a distinct entity. The Byzantine Empire holds southern Italy, Greece, and Anatolia. Anglo-Saxon England is fragmented into multiple kingdoms (Wessex, Mercia, Northumbria, East Anglia). Muslim Al-Andalus controls most of Iberia.

**~850 AD.** The Treaty of Verdun (843) has split the Carolingian Empire into three — West Francia (roughly modern France), East Francia (roughly modern Germany), and Middle Francia / Lotharingia (a strip from the Low Countries through Burgundy to Italy). The three kingdoms are the key feature — if the Frankish realm is one entity it's before 843, if it's three it's after. Viking raids are destabilizing the coastlines. Anglo-Saxon England is still fragmented.

**~900 AD.** Lotharingia has further fragmented — the Kingdom of Italy, the Kingdom of Burgundy, and Lotharingia proper are splitting apart. East and West Francia are recognizable but the Carolingian dynasty is weakening in both. Alfred the Great has unified much of England under Wessex (886). The Great Heathen Army has established the Danelaw in England. The Magyars are raiding into central Europe from the east.

**~1000 AD.** The Holy Roman Empire has replaced East Francia (Otto I crowned 962). West Francia is now recognizably the Kingdom of France under the Capetian dynasty (987). England is unified. The Danelaw is gone — but Cnut's North Sea Empire (England, Denmark, Norway) may appear on maps from ~1016–1035. The Papal States are well established. The Reconquista has pushed the Christian kingdoms of Iberia southward. Hungary and Poland are newly Christianized kingdoms.

## Future Expert Deck: Italian States, ~1494–1559

Not recommended for v1. The Italian Wars era is more sadism than gameplay — Milan alone changes hands between the Sforzas, the French, and the Spanish multiple times, Naples flips between Angevin and Aragonese claimants, the Papal States expand or contract depending on which pope has which nephew, and Florence oscillates between republic and Medici rule. Every treaty lasts about eighteen months. A player would need to know not just that the French held Milan, but *which time* the French held Milan. Worth revisiting as a curated "Machiavelli Difficulty" expert deck once themed decks are implemented. The reveal text alone — explaining which alliance just collapsed and why — would be the real draw.
